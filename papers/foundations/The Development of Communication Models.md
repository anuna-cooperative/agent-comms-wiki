# The Development of Communication Models

**Reference:** Kuznar, L. A. & Yager, M. (2020). *The Development of Communication Models (Quick Look)*. NSI, Inc., prepared for the Department of Defense Strategic Multilayer Assessment (SMA) — *Integrating Information in Joint Operations (IIJO)* project. October 2020. DTIC accession AD1118283. [DTIC citation](https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/citations/AD1118283) · [PDF](https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/pdfs/AD1118283.pdf) · [NSI](https://www.nsiteam.com/social/the-development-of-communication-models).

## Summary
This SMA "Quick Look" traces the intellectual history of **communication models** — the schematic diagrams that decompose the act of communication into elements (sender, message, channel, receiver, noise, feedback, context) and the relations among them. Its purpose is applied rather than theoretical: the review supplies the conceptual baseline for SMA's *Effective Communication Framework*, which the Department of Defense uses to reason about the **Information Environment (IE)** and about how "information influences behavior through the process of communication." Understanding the models is presented as a prerequisite for building meaningful measures of effectiveness (MOEs) for influence and information operations, rather than mere measures of performance (MOPs).

The report organises the field as a progression through three families. **Linear models** (e.g. Lasswell's 1948 "who says what to whom in what channel with what effect", and the Shannon–Weaver sender→encoder→channel→decoder→receiver-with-noise diagram derived from [[A Mathematical Theory of Communication]]) treat communication as a one-directional transmission from a source to a passive audience. **Interactive models** (e.g. Schramm/Osgood) add *feedback* and a shared *field of experience*, recognising that sender and receiver alternate roles and that a message is only understood insofar as their interpretive frames overlap. **Transactional models** go further, treating the parties as *simultaneous* senders-and-receivers who co-construct meaning within social, relational, and cultural context, so that "the information environment" itself expands as each new model admits more variables and interactions. The authors read this trajectory as a steady enrichment: successive models internalise more of the context that determines whether a message actually changes an audience's perceptions, attitudes, and ultimately behaviour.

## Key Ideas
- **Communication models as decomposition tools:** the lines, arrows, and shapes each stand for a distinct element or interaction, letting analysts isolate and measure parts of the communication process.
- **Three-stage historical arc:** *linear* (transmission) → *interactive* (feedback + shared field of experience) → *transactional* (simultaneous, context-embedded co-construction of meaning).
- **Lasswell's model:** the compact "who / says what / in which channel / to whom / with what effect" formulation of one-way mass communication.
- **Shannon–Weaver model:** the engineering transmission diagram (source, transmitter, channel, noise, receiver, destination) adapted from information theory into human communication.
- **Interactive turn (Schramm/Osgood):** feedback loops and overlapping *fields of experience* make shared meaning, not mere transmission, the criterion of success.
- **Transactional turn:** parties send and receive at once; relationship, culture, and situation are constitutive, not incidental.
- **Applied framing:** models underpin SMA's Effective Communication Framework and the shift from MOPs to MOEs when assessing influence in the Information Environment.

## Conceptual Contribution
- **Claim:** Communication is best understood not as one-way transmission but as an increasingly context-rich, bidirectional, meaning-constructing transaction; the more variables of the information environment a model captures, the better it predicts whether communication actually shifts behaviour.
- **Mechanism:** A historical survey of canonical models (Lasswell, Shannon–Weaver, Schramm/Osgood, transactional) that decomposes each into its elements and reads their succession as progressive enrichment of the represented context; the synthesis feeds SMA's Effective Communication Framework and its MOP→MOE distinction.
- **Concepts introduced/used:** [[Information Environment]], [[Influence Operations]], [[Transactional Communication]], [[Feedback]], [[Shared Field of Experience]], [[Measures of Effectiveness]]
- **Stance:** survey / applied policy framework
- **Relates to:** grounds the human-communication side of the same transmission problem Shannon formalises in [[A Mathematical Theory of Communication]]; the "meaning is co-constructed in context" thesis parallels the intent- and convention-based accounts of meaning that motivate agent communication languages (e.g. [[Communicative Actions for Artificial Agents - Cohen Levesque]]).
