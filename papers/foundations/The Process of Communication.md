# The Process of Communication

**Reference:** Berlo, D. K. (1960). *The Process of Communication: An Introduction to Theory and Practice.* New York: Holt, Rinehart and Winston. (David Kenneth Berlo.) [OpenLibrary](https://openlibrary.org/works/OL3915860W/The_process_of_communication) · [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/processofcommuni0000berl) · LCCN 60-11599.

## Summary
Berlo's *The Process of Communication* is the book that popularised the **SMCR model** — **S**ource → **M**essage → **C**hannel → **R**eceiver — as a teaching framework for human communication. Written for students of speech and communication rather than engineers, it recasts the transmission diagram of [[A Mathematical Theory of Communication|Shannon–Weaver]] in explicitly behavioural, human terms: each of the four elements is decomposed into the human factors that govern communicative success. The **source** and the **receiver** are each analysed along the same five dimensions — communication skills, attitudes, knowledge, social system, and culture — while the **message** is decomposed into content, code, treatment, elements, and structure, and the **channel** is identified with the five senses (seeing, hearing, touching, smelling, tasting).

The book's central and most-quoted thesis is that communication is a **process** — dynamic, ongoing, continuous, without a fixed beginning or end — rather than a discrete act of transmission. From this Berlo draws the receiver-centred slogan that **"meanings are in people, not in words"**: words and other signals do not *contain* meaning but merely elicit it, so a message is only as good as the meaning the receiver reconstructs. Communicative success is therefore framed as **fidelity** — the degree of match between the meaning the source intends and the meaning the receiver arrives at — and Berlo stresses that fidelity depends on the overlap of the encoder's and decoder's experience, skills, and attitudes rather than on the fidelity of the physical channel alone. The account sits historically between the linear Shannon–Weaver model and the later interactive/transactional models, foregrounding shared meaning and the human variables that condition it.

## Key Ideas
- **SMCR model:** the four-part decomposition Source → Message → Channel → Receiver as an introductory framework for analysing any communication event.
- **Source/receiver factors (the same five):** communication skills, attitudes, knowledge, social system, and culture — the human variables that shape both encoding and decoding.
- **Message factors:** content, code, treatment, and the interaction of elements and structure.
- **Channel = the senses:** the message travels through one or more of seeing, hearing, touching, smelling, and tasting.
- **Communication as *process*:** dynamic, continuous, ever-changing, with no true beginning or end — not a one-shot transmission.
- **"Meanings are in people":** signals elicit rather than carry meaning; interpretation is reconstructed by the receiver.
- **Fidelity as the success criterion:** high-fidelity communication is a match between intended and reconstructed meaning, conditioned by the overlap of source and receiver experience.

## Conceptual Contribution
- **Claim:** Communication is an ongoing *process*, not a discrete transmission, and its success is measured by *fidelity of shared meaning* — meaning that lives in people, not in signals — so the human factors of source and receiver (skills, attitudes, knowledge, social system, culture) are the primary determinants of whether communication works.
- **Mechanism:** The SMCR model decomposes any communication event into Source, Message, Channel, and Receiver, and further decomposes each element into its behavioural sub-factors, giving students an analytic checklist for diagnosing communicative breakdown as a mismatch of experience rather than a channel failure.
- **Concepts introduced/used:** [[SMCR Model]], [[Fidelity]], [[Meaning in People]], [[Sender-Receiver Framework]], [[Shared Field of Experience]]
- **Stance:** foundational / behavioural communication theory (textbook)
- **Relates to:** humanises and behaviouralises the engineering transmission model of [[A Mathematical Theory of Communication]]; sits on the *linear → interactive* boundary of the arc surveyed in [[The Development of Communication Models]], anticipating the feedback- and shared-meaning-centred interactive models with its "meanings are in people" thesis.
